## What Is Pan-Africanism?

Pan-Africanism advocates for unity among all people of African descent, recognising that colonialism and slavery created shared oppression requiring coordinated resistance. It encompasses anti-colonial struggle, continental unity, diaspora solidarity, and African socialism.

## Historical Context

Emerging in the late 19th century among diaspora intellectuals, pan-Africanism gained momentum through figures like W.E.B. Du Bois, Marcus Garvey, and later Kwame Nkrumah and Julius Nyerere. The Organisation of African Unity (now African Union) institutionalised pan-African cooperation.

## Key Dimensions

- Continental political and economic integration
- Solidarity with Black liberation struggles globally
- Cultural affirmation against colonial degradation
- Economic cooperation resisting neocolonial extraction
- Support for remaining liberation movements

## Major Expressions

- Anti-apartheid solidarity movements
- African Union and regional economic communities
- Cultural movements celebrating African heritage
- Support for civil rights and Black Power struggles
- Opposition to Western military intervention

## Relationship to Other Concepts

Pan-Africanism advances [decolonisation](/compendium/decolonisation) and [anti-imperialism](/compendium/anti-imperialism) through continental solidarity. It influenced [tricontinentalism](/compendium/tricontinentalism) and connects to [internationalism](/compendium/internationalism). Many leaders combined pan-Africanism with [socialism](/compendium/socialism), exemplified by [Sankara's Burkina Faso](/compendium/sankaras-burkina-faso) and [ujamaa](/compendium/ujamaa) in Tanzania.
